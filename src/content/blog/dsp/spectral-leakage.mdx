The DFT assumes the $N$-point signal is one period of an infinitely periodic sequence.
When the signal frequency is not an exact integer multiple of $\Delta f = f_s / N$,
this assumption is violated and energy "leaks" from the true frequency bin into neighboring bins.
This is **spectral leakage**.

## Why It Happens

Selecting $N$ samples is equivalent to multiplying an infinite signal by a rectangular window.
In the frequency domain, multiplication becomes convolution with the window's spectrum — a sinc function:

$$W_\text{rect}(f) = \frac{\sin(\pi f N / f_s)}{\sin(\pi f / f_s)}$$

The sinc has high sidelobes (first sidelobe at −13 dB). Any spectral component leaks into
adjacent bins through these sidelobes.

## The window trade-off

| Window | First sidelobe | Main lobe width |
|---|---|---|
| Rectangular | −13 dB | $2/N$ |
| Hann | −32 dB | $4/N$ |
| Hamming | −43 dB | $4/N$ |
| Blackman | −58 dB | $6/N$ |

Better sidelobe suppression always costs main lobe width — you cannot have both. Choose based on whether you care more about resolving closely-spaced frequencies (use rectangular or Hann) or detecting weak signals next to strong ones (use Blackman).

## Practical rule

If your signal frequency is known to sit exactly on a DFT bin, windowing adds nothing. In practice it rarely does, so windowing is almost always worth it. Hann is the default choice for most applications.
